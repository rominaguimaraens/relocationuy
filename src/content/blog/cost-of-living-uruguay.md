# What Moving to Uruguay Really Costs (Beyond the Visa Fees)

Cost of living guides for Uruguay tend to fall into one of two categories: the optimistic version that makes everything sound like a bargain, and the vague version that gives you ranges so wide they're useless. Neither one actually helps you build a budget.

This guide is based on what we see regularly with clients settling into Montevideo's most popular expat neighborhoods — Pocitos, Punta Carretas, Buceo, Malvín, and Carrasco. The numbers are current, the surprises are included, and the sample budgets at the end are built from real situations rather than best-case scenarios.

---

## Housing: What You'll Actually Pay in Each Neighborhood

Rent in Montevideo is quoted in US dollars almost universally, which makes budgeting straightforward. Prices vary significantly by neighborhood, building age, and whether the apartment is furnished.

**Pocitos** is the most popular neighborhood for expats — beachfront access, good infrastructure, walkable, and well-served by transit. It's also not the cheapest option.

- 1-bedroom: $700-1,000/month
- 2-bedroom: $1,000-1,400/month
- 3-bedroom: $1,400-1,800/month

**Punta Carretas** sits next to the Punta Carretas Shopping mall and has a quieter, slightly more upscale feel than Pocitos. It's a short walk to the rambla and tends to attract families.

- 1-bedroom: $800-1,200/month
- 2-bedroom: $1,200-1,600/month
- 3-bedroom: $1,600-2,200/month

**Buceo** is immediately east of Pocitos and significantly more affordable for comparable space. The tradeoff is slightly fewer amenities within walking distance, though the difference is minor.

- 1-bedroom: $600-900/month
- 2-bedroom: $900-1,200/month
- 3-bedroom: $1,200-1,600/month

**Malvín** is further east and quieter — more residential, less touristy, good for families. Lower prices than the central neighborhoods without sacrificing safety or quality of life.

- 1-bedroom: $600-850/month
- 2-bedroom: $850-1,150/month
- 3-bedroom: $1,150-1,500/month

**Carrasco** is Montevideo's most upscale residential area — large houses, tree-lined streets, close to the airport. Popular with families who want space and quiet. Also the most expensive.

- 1-bedroom: $900-1,300/month
- 2-bedroom: $1,300-1,800/month
- 3-bedroom: $1,800-2,500/month

Furnished apartments run roughly 20-30% higher than the figures above across all neighborhoods.

### Gastos Comunes: The Fee Nobody Warns You About

This is the single most common surprise for people renting in Montevideo for the first time. If you rent in a building with amenities — doorman, elevator, gym, pool, or even just shared hallways and a garden — you'll pay gastos comunes on top of your rent. This is the monthly building maintenance fee and it covers staff salaries, elevator maintenance, cleaning of common areas, shared water, and sometimes gas for building-wide hot water.

Depending on the building, gastos comunes runs anywhere from $80 to $250 per month. In a newer building with a gym and a doorman in Pocitos, expect the higher end of that range.

Landlords don't always volunteer this information. Before you sign anything, ask directly: *¿Cuánto son los gastos comunes?* Add that number to your monthly rent when you're comparing apartments.

### What You'll Need to Move In

Moving into an apartment in Uruguay requires more upfront cash than people typically expect. You're looking at one to two months' rent as a refundable security deposit, plus your first month's rent. Some landlords also require a garantía — essentially a financial guarantee against default. This can come from a third party who owns property in Uruguay, or through a bank guarantee arranged through companies like Contaduría, which typically costs a one-time fee of around $500-800.

Total cash needed to move in: plan for the equivalent of two to three months' rent.

## Utilities: Month by Month

### Electricity (UTE)

This is the line item that surprises people most in winter. Uruguay relies heavily on electric heating, and if you heat your apartment electrically during the winter months (June through August), your bill will be substantially higher than the rest of the year.

- Summer (December-March): $40-80/month for a 2-bedroom, driven up by air conditioning
- Winter (June-September): $80-150/month for a 2-bedroom with electric heating
- Spring/Fall: $30-60/month

Many locals use gas heaters or simply layer clothing rather than run electric heating constantly. If your tolerance for cold is low and you plan to keep the apartment warm all winter, factor the higher end of the winter range into your budget.

### Water (OSE)

$15-30/month. Very affordable and sometimes included in gastos comunes.

### Gas

If your building uses supergas bottles for cooking or water heating, expect $20-40/month. Some buildings have central gas included in the gastos comunes.

### Internet

Fiber internet from Antel, Movistar, or Claro costs $30-60/month and is reliable in all the neighborhoods listed above. Speeds of 100-300 Mbps are standard. This is not a category where Uruguay cuts corners.

### Mobile Phone

Prepaid plans run $10-20/month for basic usage. Postpaid plans with unlimited calls, texts, and data come in around $25-50/month. Any provider will sell you a SIM with just your passport.

**Total monthly utilities for a couple in a 2-bedroom:** $150-300, depending heavily on season and whether you're heating electrically in winter.

## Healthcare: What It Actually Costs and How It Works

Uruguay's healthcare system has two tracks: public and private. The public system (ASSE) is free but navigating it as a newcomer is more complex, and wait times are longer. Most expats use the private system — a mutualista, which is a cooperative health model that covers the vast majority of medical needs for a monthly fee.

**Monthly mutualista costs:**

- Individual under 40: $80-120/month
- Individual 40-60: $120-180/month
- Couple: $180-280/month
- Family with two children: $300-450/month

Well-regarded providers include Médica Uruguaya, CASMU, Círculo Católico, and the British Hospital. Coverage includes general practitioner visits, specialists, prescriptions, hospital stays, and emergency care. Co-pays are minimal — $0-5 for a general appointment, $5-15 for a specialist, $2-20 for prescriptions depending on the medication.

The one thing to know before you enroll: pre-existing conditions may have a waiting period before coverage kicks in, typically three to twelve months depending on the condition and provider. If you have ongoing medical needs, look into this before choosing your plan and provider.

## Groceries: Feria vs. Supermarket

Uruguay produces excellent quality beef, dairy, produce, and bread, and local food is reasonably priced. Imported goods — anything with a recognizable international brand, anything that has to cross the Atlantic — are expensive, often two to three times what you'd pay for the same item in the US.

### Weekly grocery costs for a couple:

- Supermarket only (Disco, Tienda Inglesa, Géant): $120-180/week, or roughly $480-720/month
- Mix of feria and supermarket: $80-120/week, or $320-480/month
- Mostly home cooking, vegetarian-leaning: $60-100/week, or $240-400/month

The feria — the outdoor produce market that operates in most neighborhoods on a weekly schedule — is where you want to buy produce. Prices are lower than supermarkets and the quality is often better. Finding your neighborhood feria and building it into your weekly routine is one of the more immediate ways to reduce your grocery spending.

### What things actually cost:

At the feria:
- Tomatoes: $1-2 per kilo
- Lettuce: around $1
- Bananas: $1.50 per kilo
- Avocados: $2-4 each depending on season
- Fresh bread: $1-2

At the supermarket:
- Milk (1 liter): $1.50
- Eggs (dozen): $3-4
- Chicken breast (1 kilo): $7-9
- Ground beef (1 kilo): $8-12
- Pasta (500g): $2-3
- Local wine (decent bottle): $5-12
- Beer (6-pack, local brand): $6-8

Imported goods:
- Peanut butter: $8-12
- Maple syrup: $15-20
- American cereals: $10-15

If specific imported products are part of your daily life, factor this in. Most people adapt to local equivalents within a few months and stop missing the imported versions. Some don't, and that's fine — just budget for it.

Coffee at a café runs $3-4 for a café con leche, and medialunas (the local croissant equivalent) are $1-1.50 each. These are not things you need to give up to live affordably here.

## Transportation

Most expats living in Pocitos, Punta Carretas, or Buceo don't own cars. The neighborhoods are walkable, the rambla makes cycling pleasant, and a combination of the STM bus system and occasional Uber covers everything else.

**Public bus (STM card):** $1.60 per ride, rechargeable card. A monthly budget of $50-60 covers daily bus use.

**Uber/taxi:**
- Short ride within the neighborhood: $4-6
- Pocitos to Ciudad Vieja: $8-12
- Airport to Pocitos: $25-35

**Owning a car:**
- Used car purchase: $8,000-15,000
- Gas: around $1.80 per liter (roughly $6.80 per gallon)
- Insurance: $60-150/month
- Annual registration (patente): $300-800

For families in Malvín or Carrasco, or anyone with kids in school outside walking distance, a car becomes more practical. For most single people and couples in the central neighborhoods, it's an expense that doesn't pay off.

## Dining Out and Social Life

**Restaurants:**
- Menú del día (set lunch, typically three courses): $8-12
- Mid-range dinner for two: $40-70
- Nice restaurant, dinner for two: $80-120
- Pizza delivery: $12-18

Uruguay's restaurant culture centers on lunch more than dinner — the menú del día is genuinely good in most places and represents excellent value. Dinner out is a leisurely event rather than a quick meal.

**Other:**
- Gym membership: $30-60/month
- Movie ticket: $8-10
- Yoga class (drop-in): $8-12
- Coworking space (monthly): $80-150

## The Costs That Catch People Off Guard

**Gastos comunes** — covered above, but worth repeating because it catches almost everyone. Always ask before you sign.

**Winter electricity** — if you're used to gas heating, the cost of keeping an apartment warm electrically in a Uruguayan winter is a genuine shock. The winter months are June, July, and August. Plan for it.

**Furniture** — there is no IKEA in Uruguay. Renting unfurnished and furnishing from scratch typically costs $2,000-5,000 depending on how much you need and how long you're willing to shop secondhand. Facebook Marketplace and secondhand stores (there are good ones in Pocitos and Punta Carretas) bring this number down significantly if you have time to hunt.

**Cash** — ferias, many corner stores, and some smaller restaurants still prefer cash. Uruguay is more cashless than it used to be, but keeping pesos on hand saves friction for everyday purchases.

**Banking setup time** — opening a local bank account takes longer and requires more documentation than people expect. Budget extra time for this step and don't assume you can handle it in your first week.

## Is Uruguay Cheaper Than Living in the US?

It depends almost entirely on where you're comparing to and how you live.

Compared to major US cities — New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Boston, Seattle — Uruguay is substantially cheaper for equivalent quality of life. A comfortable lifestyle in Pocitos that would cost $5,000-6,000 per month in those cities runs closer to $2,500-3,500 here.

Compared to mid-size US cities like Nashville, Austin, or Raleigh, Uruguay is similar or slightly cheaper depending on your lifestyle choices.

Compared to small-town America, Uruguay can feel comparable or even slightly more expensive in some categories, particularly for housing in certain US regions.

The categories where Uruguay is clearly cheaper: healthcare, dining out, fresh produce, public transportation, and the overall tax and cost burden on daily life.

The categories where Uruguay is not cheaper: imported goods, electronics and appliances (buying locally is expensive — most expats bring what they can or buy secondhand), private schooling, and furnished rental housing.

Remote workers keeping US or European salaries live very comfortably here. Local employment means adapting your expectations because Uruguayan professional salaries are modest by US standards, typically $1,500-2,500 per month for established professionals, which is livable in Uruguay but a significant adjustment if you're used to more.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Cost of Living in Uruguay

**How much money do I need to live comfortably in Montevideo as a single person?**
A comfortable lifestyle in a central neighborhood with regular dining out, a private health plan, and reasonable social spending runs around $1,800-2,200 per month. You can live on less if you cook at home, take public transit, and choose Buceo or Malvín over Pocitos. You'll spend more if you eat out frequently or live in Punta Carretas or Carrasco.

**Is Montevideo cheaper than Buenos Aires?**
It's comparable, and in some categories more expensive. Buenos Aires has much lower prices at the moment due to economic conditions, but financial volatility there creates its own costs. For stable, predictable budgeting, Uruguay is the more reliable option.

**How much does private health insurance cost in Uruguay for a foreigner?**
Mutualista plans for individuals under 40 typically run $80-120 per month. For people 40-60, expect $120-180. Couples pay $180-280 and families of four around $300-450. These cover the vast majority of medical needs with low co-pays.

**What is a gastos comunes fee in Uruguay?**
It's the monthly building maintenance fee paid by residents of apartment buildings. It covers shared staff, elevator maintenance, common area cleaning, and sometimes building-wide utilities. It ranges from $80-250 per month depending on the building and is paid in addition to rent. Always ask about this before signing a lease.

**Do I need a car to live in Montevideo?**
In the central expat neighborhoods (Pocitos, Punta Carretas, Buceo), no. The bus system is reliable, walking is practical, and Uber covers longer distances. Families in outer neighborhoods or with school-age children often find a car more practical, but it's not essential in the city center.

**How much does it cost to furnish an apartment in Uruguay?**
Furnishing from scratch in an unfurnished rental typically costs $2,000-5,000 depending on your standards and how much time you have to shop secondhand. There's no IKEA. Good secondhand options exist, particularly on Facebook Marketplace and in vintage shops in the central neighborhoods.

**Are imported products expensive in Uruguay?**
Yes, significantly. Anything that doesn't originate in Uruguay or the immediate region carries a substantial import premium. Specific American brands, processed foods, and electronics can cost two to three times the US price. Most people adapt to local equivalents without much difficulty after the initial adjustment period.
